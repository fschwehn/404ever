Column I · The First
108 Minutes That Changed Everything

On the morning of April 12, 1961, a 27-year-old Soviet Air Force pilot named Yuri Gagarin sat inside a sphere barely wider than a closet and let the Earth fall away beneath him. Vostok 1 completed a single orbit in 108 minutes. Gagarin returned.

He called out his famous words over the radio as the cosmodrome shrank below him. The world — both sides of it — stopped to listen. That day is now International Day of Human Space Flight, marked by the United Nations and celebrated on every continent as Yuri's Night.

The Sixty-Five Year Chain

From Gagarin's single orbit to the Space Shuttle's first flight on this same date in 1981 — another April 12 — to today, when four humans have only just returned from lunar distance: the chain is unbroken. Over 600 people from more than 40 countries have followed in Gagarin's wake.

The first flight lasted under two hours. The most recent, ten days. The farthest reach: 252,756 miles from home — the greatest distance any human being has ever stood from Earth.

Column II · The Return
They Came Home Two Days Ago

Orion Integrity touched the Pacific on April 10, 2026, at 8:07 p.m. EDT — barely 40 miles off San Diego. The blackout lasted six minutes. Then: parachutes. Then: the water. Commander Reid Wiseman radioed Houston: "four crew green."

Christina Koch became the first woman to travel to lunar distance. Victor Glover, the first person of colour to do so. Jeremy Hansen, the first non-American. All four emerged from the capsule into the California sunlight having traveled 700,237 miles, slowing from 24,661 mph through 2,700°C of re-entry fire.

What Comes Next

Engineers are already back at Kennedy Space Center preparing the launch platform for Artemis III, which will place humans on the lunar surface for the first time since Apollo 17 in 1972. Gagarin orbited once. These four orbited the Moon.

"We did it," said NASA's Lori Glaze. "To the generation that now knows what we're capable of: welcome to our moonshot."

The Solar Eclipse, From the Far Side

On April 6, Victor Glover watched the sun disappear behind the Moon from the other side of it. He called it the greatest gift of the mission. They also photographed a crescent Earth rising above the lunar horizon — their own Earthrise, in 2026.

Column III · This World
Hungary Votes Today

While the world marks Yuri's Night, Hungary holds what may be its most consequential election in a generation. After 16 years in power, Prime Minister Viktor Orbán faces challenger Péter Magyar of the Tisza Party, who emerged from inside the system as a voice against corruption. Polls disagree sharply; results are expected tonight.

The outcome will shape Europe's posture on Ukraine, Russia, and the meaning of democratic governance within the EU. A world anxiously watching elections while its astronauts come home from the Moon.

Islamabad: No Agreement

The US-Iran talks in Islamabad concluded without specific agreements. Vice President Vance's delegation has left Pakistan. No date for a second round has been set. The Strait of Hormuz remains cautiously open; crude oil has retreated from its peak.

Ukraine: Easter Truce Holds

Putin's Easter ceasefire, declared from 16:00 Moscow time on April 11, holds through today. Zelensky confirmed Ukraine's readiness. The silence is fragile. France prepares for war, its chief of armed forces says.

Artemis III: Already in Motion

"No rest for the weary," said John Giles at Kennedy Space Center, already moving to reassemble the rocket for the Moon landing mission. The core stage fuel tank arrives later this month. Gagarin would have turned 92 in March.
