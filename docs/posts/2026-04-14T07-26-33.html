The Chokepoint
The Strait
Goes Dark
The US military blockade of Iran's ports took effect yesterday at 10 a.m. ET. Today the world wakes to oil above $100 and a waterway that carries a fifth of the world's energy standing nearly still.

The Strait of Hormuz — 34 kilometers at its narrowest, the hinge of the global energy economy — became the day's central theater. At 10 a.m. Eastern Time Monday, the US military formalized what it is calling a "blockade" of all Iranian ports and coastal areas, following the collapse of the weekend's Islamabad peace talks after 21 hours of negotiations without a breakthrough.

Vice President Vance, departing Islamabad, said Iran chose "not to accept our terms." Iran's armed forces called the blockade piracy, as thousands rallied in Tehran against the action. Iranian officials said the country's ports are "either for everyone or for no one."

The action is more limited than Trump's initial announcement suggested. CENTCOM clarified that the blockade applies to vessels entering or departing Iranian ports — it will not prevent ships from transiting the strait to and from non-Iranian ports. Still, traffic through the waterway is at an effective standstill, with roughly 31 vessels crossing in the last 24 hours — a fraction of normal volume.

Global reaction fractured along familiar lines. France's Macron announced a "peaceful multinational mission" to restore freedom of navigation in the strait, coordinating with Britain. A senior NATO official told CBS News that the UK is leading planning for a coalition of "more than 40 nations." China's Foreign Minister Wang Yi said the blockade does not serve the world's "common interests." The UK declined to participate in the US action itself.

The ceasefire with Iran is "holding well," Trump said Tuesday — but added he "doesn't care" whether Iran returns to talks. Later in the day, he told reporters that Iranian officials had called and "want to work a deal." Pakistan is reportedly offering to host another round.

Post-Election · Budapest
New Hungary
Begins Moving
Two days after the landslide, Péter Magyar holds his first press conference as Prime Minister-elect and names his first demands.

On Monday, the day after his historic supermajority win, Péter Magyar convened a press conference in Budapest. His first move: calling on Hungarian President Tamás Sulyok — appointed by Orbán — to resign immediately after asking the winner to form a government. He sent the same message to judges, court presidents, and media regulators installed by Fidesz over 16 years in power. "I call on all the puppets who have been in power for the past 16 years to do the same," he said.

Magyar, 45, said he plans to visit Brussels to unlock billions in frozen EU funds, and pledged to restore Hungary's independent checks and balances, join the European Public Prosecutor's Office, and reduce the country's dependence on Russian energy by 2035.

He called on parliament to convene as early as May 5. He told a crowd of cheering supporters Sunday that Poland would be his first foreign trip as PM — a pointed symbol of re-alignment with Central European democratic partners.

Global reaction continued rolling in through Monday and Tuesday. US Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer framed the result as a warning to Trump ahead of the midterms. Former President Obama called it "a victory for democracy, not just in Europe, but around the world." Italy's Meloni — one of Orbán's loudest European backers — congratulated Magyar on his "clear election victory."

Trump had not publicly weighed in as of Tuesday morning, despite his vocal pre-election endorsements of Orbán and a campaign visit from Vice President Vance to Budapest days before the vote.

Dr. B. R. Ambedkar · Born April 14, 1891
Educate, Agitate, Organise!
— B. R. Ambedkar · Chief Architect, Constitution of India

Ambedkar Jayanti — also known as Equality Day — observes the 135th birth anniversary of Dr. Bhimrao Ramji Ambedkar, jurist, economist, social reformer, and chief architect of the Indian Constitution. Indian equity markets are closed today. Banks are shut in most major cities. Public life pauses to remember.

Ambedkar was born on April 14, 1891, in Mhow, Madhya Pradesh — the 14th child of a military officer, born into the Mahar community, then considered untouchable. He earned degrees from Columbia University and the London School of Economics, became independent India's first Law Minister, and chaired the Constitution Drafting Committee.

Today is also Baisakhi, the Sikh harvest festival — a day where two tributaries of justice and abundance converge in the same April sun. Today is the blue flag and the golden field at once.

April 14 carries the world's weight quietly: Titanic sank into the North Atlantic on this night in 1912. Lincoln was shot on this evening in 1865. And today, Hungary's new government takes shape, the Strait of Hormuz holds its breath, and a billion-plus people in India remember the man who told them their rights were theirs by right, not by gift.
